# Project Brief — Reference Structure

This is the exact section-by-section structure every project brief must follow. Each section includes its purpose and what kind of content belongs there.

---

## 1. Title Block

```
[PROJECT NAME]  |  Project Brief
Version [X.X]  ·  [Month Year]  ·  Owner: [Name]
```

The title is the product/project name. Version starts at 1.0. Owner is the person responsible for this brief.

---

## 2. PROJECT AT A GLANCE

A quick-reference table with 5–7 key metadata fields. This lets a reader grasp the project's state in 10 seconds.

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Status | Current phase + one-line description (e.g., "Pre-build — Concept validated, no code yet") |
| Pilot client | Name + target date + scale |
| Early client | Name + target date + scale (if applicable) |
| Lead contact | Person + their role in the project |
| Next milestone | Specific action + date |
| Indicative value | Budget range or initial engagement value |

Adapt the fields to fit the project. Not every project has a pilot client or indicative value — use what's relevant.

---

## 3. HOW THIS CAME ABOUT

A narrative section (3–5 paragraphs) that tells the origin story:

- **Who and why**: Who initiated this? What's their background? Why are they the right person?
- **The gap**: What problem or opportunity did they identify? What's missing in the market?
- **Evidence of demand**: Why is this not speculative? What signals exist that people want this?
- **The partnership**: How did the builder (your company) get involved? What's each party's role?

If there are specific client opportunities, add sub-sections:

### CLIENT OPPORTUNITY 1 — [LABEL]
**[Client Name]**
One paragraph: who they are, what the engagement looks like, why it matters.

### CLIENT OPPORTUNITY 2 — [LABEL]
**[Client Name]**
One paragraph: who they are, what the engagement looks like, why it matters.

---

## 4. WHAT WE ARE BUILDING

The product description. This is the heart of the brief. Structure:

**Opening paragraph**: What is the product at a high level? What makes it different from what exists?

**Core thesis**: What does the product NOT replace? What does it enable? (e.g., "A skilled human is still required — the AI gives them superpowers")

**Product layers/architecture**: Describe the product's conceptual layers — not implementation details, but the logical components that shape the user experience. Typically 2–4 layers.

**Physical/practical setup**: How does this actually work in practice? What does the user see, touch, interact with?

### A [SCENARIO] IN PRACTICE — WHAT THE TEAM IS BUILDING TOWARD

A vivid, narrative walkthrough (4–6 paragraphs) describing what the product looks like in actual use. Written in present tense, from the perspective of someone watching it happen. Include:
- The setting and participants
- The product doing its job (specific moments, not abstractions)
- Moments where the product adds unexpected value
- The outcome — what does the user walk away with?

This section sells the vision. It should feel real and specific, not like a feature list.

---

## 5. WHO WE ARE BUILDING FOR

Identify the distinct user types (typically 2–3). For each:

### [USER TYPE LABEL] — [ROLE IN THE SYSTEM]
## [Short title]

#### WHO THEY ARE
- 3–5 bullet points describing demographics, experience level, technical comfort
- The first user of this type (if known) as a concrete example

#### WHAT THEY ARE TRYING TO DO
- 4–6 bullet points describing their goals, not features
- Written from their perspective ("Deliver...", "Retain...", "Feel...")

#### THEIR DEFINITION OF SUCCESS
- 2–4 bullet points — how they'd describe a win

After listing user types, add a clarifying note about the business model: who is the customer vs. the end user? This affects feature prioritization.

---

## 6. THE OPPORTUNITY — WHERE WE SIT ON THE SPECTRUM

A 3-column positioning framework showing:

| WHERE MOST ARE TODAY | WHERE WE BUILD | WHERE WE AIM |
|---|---|---|
| Current state of the market — what exists, what's commoditized | Our initial position — what we do differently | The north star — where this heads if successful |

Each column gets a short label + 1–2 sentence description.

---

## 7. HOW THE PRODUCT WORKS — [N] LAYERS

A structured breakdown of the product's conceptual architecture. For each layer:

### LAYER [N]
**[Layer Name]**
One paragraph describing what this layer does and why it matters.

This should mirror the layers described in Section 4 but in a more structured, scannable format.

---

## 8. KEY DATES AND CLIENT TIMELINES

A timeline table:

| Date | Milestone | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| [Date/Range] | [Milestone name] | [One-line description] |

List dates chronologically. Include both internal milestones and client-facing deadlines.

---

## 9. WHAT SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE — MVP

3–6 success criteria, each with:

**[Criterion Name]**
One paragraph describing what "done" looks like for this criterion. Be specific — include numbers, dates, and observable outcomes where possible.

---

## 10. KEY RISKS TO KEEP IN MIND

4–7 risks, each with:

**[Risk Name]**
One paragraph describing: what the risk is, why it matters, and what the current mitigation strategy is. Focus on risks that are specific to THIS project, not generic software risks.

---

## 11. IMMEDIATE NEXT STEPS

A numbered or bulleted list of 4–7 concrete actions:
- Who does what, by when
- Written as "[Person] to [action] by [date]"
- Ordered by urgency

---

## Writing Style Guidelines

- **Tone**: Professional but direct. No jargon without explanation. No marketing fluff.
- **Perspective**: Written by the building team about what they understand. Not a sales pitch.
- **Length**: 2,500–4,000 words total. Long enough to be comprehensive, short enough to be read.
- **Specificity**: Use real names, real numbers, real dates wherever available. Mark unknowns as [TO BE CONFIRMED].
- **Narrative sections** (How This Came About, What We Are Building): Write in flowing paragraphs, not bullets.
- **Reference sections** (At a Glance, Risks, Success Criteria): Use structured formats — tables, headers, short paragraphs.
