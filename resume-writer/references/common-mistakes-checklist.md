# Common Mistakes Checklist

Run through this checklist before finalizing any resume. Each item is a real mistake that causes resumes to be deprioritized or rejected.

---

## Format & Layout

- [ ] **Using two-column layout** — Switch to single-column, top-down. Two-column confuses reading order and discourages detail.
- [ ] **Inconsistent formatting** — Same font sizes, same alignment method (tabs not spaces), same bullet style throughout.
- [ ] **Too much bolding** — Only bold: section headers, job titles, company names, dates. Never bold random words mid-sentence.
- [ ] **Too "flashy" design** — Developer resumes are 95% content, 5% style. Fancy templates hurt readability. Exception: UX/design roles.
- [ ] **Using paragraphs instead of bullets** — Always use bullet points. Recruiters don't read paragraphs.
- [ ] **Sub-bullets** — Stick to one level of bullets. Sub-bullets clutter the resume.
- [ ] **Wrong file format** — Must be PDF. Not .doc, .docx, .rtf. Name it `Firstname_Lastname_Resume.pdf`.
- [ ] **Poor margins** — Don't waste space with oversized margins, but don't cram content with tiny margins either.

## Content Problems

- [ ] **No numbers** — Every bullet should have at least one number. This alone beats 90% of resumes.
- [ ] **Passive language** — Use active verbs: Led, Built, Designed, Reduced, Shipped. Not "was responsible for" or "was involved in."
- [ ] **Talking about "we" instead of "I"** — The resume is about YOUR contribution. Drop "we" and "the team."
- [ ] **Generic responsibilities instead of achievements** — "Responsible for backend services" tells nothing. "Designed and shipped 4 microservices handling 500 RPS" tells a story.
- [ ] **Internal jargon and acronyms** — Replace project codenames and company-specific acronyms with descriptions anyone can understand.
- [ ] **Clichés instead of evidence** — "Team player," "fast learner," "hit the ground running" — replace with specific examples that demonstrate these qualities.
- [ ] **"etc." or "and so on"** — Be specific or cut it. Sloppy phrasing suggests sloppy thinking.
- [ ] **Too verbose** — Ruthlessly edit. If a bullet is more than 2 lines, split or trim it.
- [ ] **Not tailored for the job** — A generic resume loses to a tailored one every time. Mirror the job description's language.

## Unnecessary Details

- [ ] **Photo** — Remove it. Creates unconscious bias. Never required for US/tech positions.
- [ ] **Personal information** — No date of birth, gender, marital status, citizenship, religion, or number of children.
- [ ] **More than 4 contact items** — Phone, email, LinkedIn, and one of GitHub/website. That's it.
- [ ] **Spoken languages section** — Remove unless applying to non-English-speaking company. Recruiters care about programming languages.
- [ ] **Self-rating skill levels** — No stars, bars, percentages, or "expert/proficient" labels. They only backfire. Just list what you're proficient with.
- [ ] **References or "references available on request"** — Waste of space. This is expected and handled after interviews.
- [ ] **Quotes from references or performance reviews** — Look out of place. Save for LinkedIn recommendations.
- [ ] **Irrelevant past jobs** — Don't list pizza delivery when you have 10+ years in tech.
- [ ] **Trivial technologies** — Don't list Word, Trello, JIRA, or tools trivial to learn.
- [ ] **Old/rusty languages** — Remove languages you haven't used in years. They can trigger age bias.
- [ ] **Generic objective statement** — "Seeking a position in software engineering" adds nothing. Remove or replace with tailored summary.
- [ ] **Cover letter** — Don't include unless sending directly to a hiring manager (rarely useful).

## Links

- [ ] **Non-clickable links** — All links must be clickable. Recruiters will never copy-paste a URL.
- [ ] **Full URLs visible** — Hide URLs behind descriptive text: `[My Project](url)` not `https://github.com/user/repo/blob/master/README.md`
- [ ] **Links to stale content** — If your GitHub is untouched for years or your website is outdated, remove the link. Stale content hurts.
- [ ] **Links standing out visually** — Don't use default blue link color. Make links same color as text with underline. Use highlight color on section names instead.
- [ ] **Too many social profiles** — No Instagram, Twitter, Skype, Upwork, Medium on top of LinkedIn and GitHub.

## Dates & Timeline

- [ ] **Dates not in reverse chronological order** — Most recent first, always.
- [ ] **Ambiguous date format** — Write "June 2021 — July 2023", not "06/21—07/23" (is that June 2021 or the 6th of the 21st?).
- [ ] **Too much precision on old dates** — Drop months for positions 3+ years ago. "2018" is fine; "March 2018" is noise.
- [ ] **Missing graduation date** — Include it. "2019—present" is not acceptable.
- [ ] **Promotions not visible** — Show title changes clearly within the same company.

## Experience Level Mistakes

- [ ] **New grad resume over 1 page** — Fit it on one page. Period.
- [ ] **10+ years and still listing coursework** — Education should be 1-2 lines at this point.
- [ ] **Senior engineer without a summary** — Add one, tailored to the role.
- [ ] **Manager resume that only lists technical achievements** — Must include people achievements: promotions facilitated, attrition rates, mentoring.
- [ ] **Hybrid IC/manager resume** — Pick one track per resume. Create separate versions.
- [ ] **Career changer not explaining the pivot** — Include a brief summary explaining your motivation.

## Keyword Strategy

- [ ] **Missing key technologies from job description** — If the job asks for "distributed systems" and you have that experience, use that exact phrase.
- [ ] **Keyword stuffing** — Include relevant keywords naturally. Don't stuff irrelevant ones.
- [ ] **Technologies only in Skills section** — Repeat key technologies in Work Experience bullets to prove hands-on usage.
- [ ] **Sensible keyword placement** — Short Skills/Technologies section + specifics woven into experience bullets = optimal for both keyword-scanning recruiters and detailed-reading hiring managers.

## The 10-Second Scan Test

Ask someone to look at your resume for 10 seconds, then answer:
1. How many years of experience does this person have?
2. What are their primary technologies?
3. Where do they currently work and what's their title?
4. What's the most impressive thing on this resume?

If they can't answer all four, restructure until they can.
