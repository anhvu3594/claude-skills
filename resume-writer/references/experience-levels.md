## College Students & New Grads

**Resume must fit on one page.** You're competing against hundreds of applicants — no one reads page 2.

### Section Priority Order
1. Work experience / internships (if any) — real-world experience is your strongest asset
2. Languages & Technologies — must be on page 1
3. Projects — use the RIC framework; link to GitHub repos with polished READMEs
4. Education — graduation date, major, GPA (only if high), awards, standout achievements
5. Interests — optional, conversation starters for interviews

### How to Stand Out
- Internships with specific results, impact, and your contribution
- Contributions to real open-source projects (not just your own repos)
- Projects with real users, impressive metrics, or notable complexity
- Tutoring, TA work, or leading student groups (shows mentoring ability)
- Add context about your school if it's prestigious: "Top 5 CS program in [country]"

### Key Advice
- Read the job description and amend your resume accordingly
- Clarify when you can start (graduation date, availability)
- Don't include an objective statement
- Only include GPA if it's high
- DO include graduation date (not "2019—present")
- Use numbers to show what you did, not generic responsibilities
- Don't waste time on cover letters unless sending directly to a hiring manager

## Bootcamp Graduates

The core challenge: limited experience and competing with many other bootcamp grads for the same positions.

### Strategy: Projects Are Your Differentiator

**1. Publish projects with excellent GitHub READMEs**
- Include summary, screenshots, how to run, how to test
- Most bootcamp projects lack this polish — doing it sets you apart

**2. Add automated tests**
- Most bootcamp grads don't include unit or end-to-end tests
- Add a "Running tests" section to your README
- Mention code coverage percentage — quantification matters

**3. Contribute to popular open-source projects**
- Getting a PR merged into a project with thousands of stars is incredibly impressive
- Nearly zero junior developers do this — it immediately stands out
- Example: "Contributed to Leaflet (28,000+ stars), the leading open-source JavaScript mapping library"
- Senior engineers reviewing your PR teach you more than most bootcamps

**4. Turn coding challenges into full projects**
- Don't stop after submitting takehome projects — polish and publish them
- If rejected, ask for feedback and build improvements into a public version
- Invest $15 in a domain and hosting to make it look production-ready

**5. Get any paid development work**
- Freelancing, even small projects, signals you can deliver in production
- Offer to help a friend or small business at discount/free — the result speaks louder than how you got it

## Career Changers

All bootcamp grad advice applies, plus:

### Resume Strategy
1. **Include an explanatory summary** — explain the career change and your motivation
2. **Front-load technical skills** — languages, projects, portfolio near the top
3. **Reframe past experience** — keep previous jobs to 1 sentence each, focus on transferable skills:
   - Proactive learning
   - Teamwork and collaboration
   - Mentoring others
   - Communication and public speaking
4. **Keep it to 1-1.5 pages** — hiring managers care about standout projects, not your previous career history

### Structure
1. Summary (explaining the pivot)
2. Technical Skills
3. Projects (your primary selling point)
4. Experience (concise, transferable skills highlighted)
5. Education (software development education detailed, other degrees brief)

## Career Breaks

### How to Handle Based on Timing

**Break was 4-5+ years ago:** Don't explain it on the resume. Handle in screening call if asked.

**Recent break, but currently employed:** Use judgment — gap is less relevant since you're working now.

**Recent break extending to present:** Tell a story. Your resume should match what you'd tell a hiring manager.

### Phrasing Examples
- Travel: "Fulfilled life-long dream of traveling through South America for six months, improving Spanish and gaining cross-cultural understanding"
- Health: "Took time off due to a health matter, now fully recovered and involved in [relevant volunteer work]"
- Caregiving: "Took time off to care for a family member, who has now fully recovered"
- Career change: "Chose to take time off to pursue [interest] and determine a new career path aligned with personal goals"

### Study-Related Breaks
Production work beats course completion every time. If you spent a break learning:
- Quantify why projects were interesting/difficult using the RIC framework
- Go beyond portfolio projects — prove mastery by shipping something in production, teaching what you learned, or contributing to open source
- "Freelancing" signals you can deliver; it's stronger than self-study alone

## Mid-Level Engineers

### Key Adjustments
- Work experience takes the top spot — it's your primary selling point
- Languages & Technologies on page 1
- Show promotions as separate sub-sections with clear title changes
- Education becomes less detailed (degree, school, date, maybe one standout achievement)
- Spend less space on positions older than 5 years
- Remove obsolete technologies from your skills list
- Personal projects only if they truly stand out

### Tailoring
- Create different versions for different types of roles
- Mirror the job description's language
- Tailor your Languages & Technologies section to match what they're hiring for

## Senior Engineers

With 8+ years, you have more experience than fits on 2 pages. The challenge is relevance, not content.

### Resume Strategies

1. **Make promotions visible** — they show you create impact and get recognized
2. **Add a tailored summary** — customize for each position; what would the company get with you?
3. **Highlight "soft" achievements** — mentoring juniors, leading teams, cross-team influence
4. **Discuss influence, not just impact** — what teams or organizations did you influence? Staff-level engineers are expected to influence multiple teams
5. **Move education to page 2** — reduces age bias; hiring manager decides on qualification before seeing graduation date
6. **Minimize education details** — school, degree, date only; keep summa cum laude, drop everything else
7. **Compress earlier work** — further in the past = less space
8. **Consider direct outreach** — message recruiters and hiring managers on LinkedIn; senior candidates get responses

### Length
- 2 pages is the guideline, not a hard rule
- Up to 3 pages is reasonable for 10+ years
- Focus on relevance over completeness

## Tech Leads

Hiring managers for tech leads have specific problems: team behind on delivery, high turnover, poor quality. They hire tech leads when adding more people won't solve the problem.

### What to Showcase

1. **Beyond-senior achievements** — Did you speed up delivery? Improve quality? Repair stakeholder relationships?
2. **Team success examples** — Track record of making teams better, with evidence
3. **Specifics with context:**
   - Team size and makeup: "Led a team of 2 front-end, 2 back-end, and 1 QA engineer"
   - Organizational context: "Part of a 50-person engineering org building..."
   - Major technical decisions you drove
4. **Both outcomes AND activities:**
   - Delivery: "Managed team that delivered project X on time and on budget"
   - Team leadership: "Coached 2 graduates who became senior engineers within 18 months"
   - Technical leadership: "Drove architecture decision for component X, adopted across 3 teams"

### Tip
Fewer tech lead positions are advertised than senior engineer or engineering manager roles. Consider maintaining separate resumes for all three tracks, highlighting relevant experience for each.

## Engineering Managers

### Resume Strategies

1. **Tell a story** — how you made teams better, achieved great results, and what you'll help the organization achieve
2. **Research company values** — engineering managers are hired for culture and team fit; reflect values you represent
3. **Strong, tailored summary** — this is effectively your cover letter; avoid buzzwords; make it specific to you
4. **Don't force fit** — only pursue conversations where you play to your strengths; be clear on environments where you deliver best
5. **Cover both business and people achievements:**
   - Shipped impactful projects, hit OKRs
   - Helped people get promoted
   - Hired for diversity
   - Low attrition on your teams
   - Coached/mentored beyond your direct reports
6. **Browse other EM LinkedIn profiles** for inspiration on how they discuss work and impact
7. **Reach out before applying** — easier as an EM to have an intro chat with recruiter or hiring manager

### Creating Separate IC vs. Manager Resumes
If you've oscillated between IC and management:
- Create one resume emphasizing the IC path
- Create another emphasizing the management path
- Don't create a generic hybrid — it gets put in the "unsure what to do with this one" pile
- Both resumes tell different true stories from the same career facts
